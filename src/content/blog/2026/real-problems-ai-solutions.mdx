There's an interesting pattern I've been noticing: **the best product ideas rarely come from complex market research**. They come from everyday frustrations that many people quietly experience — small things that make them mutter, *"someone really should fix this."*

I'm in a phase I find genuinely exciting: collecting real problems from people across the world, then figuring out whether AI can be the answer. This article is part of that process — and I'd love for you to be part of it too.

## The Pattern That Keeps Repeating

Every time a new technology emerges — including AI — it's rarely the most technically sophisticated solution that wins first. It's the one that's most precisely targeted. The one that solves a problem that's been around forever, but that nobody bothered to tackle because it seemed too small or too specific.

> "The products that endure aren't the most innovative ones — they're the most needed ones, even before people realize they need them."

And I believe: the AI tools available today have extraordinary potential to fill exactly those gaps. Not as replacements for human connection, but as bridges — in the spaces where access, time, or cost have always been barriers.

## Two Real Examples: Thesis Defense & Language Learning

### Simulating a thesis defense

Almost every university student knows the feeling: the anxiety of facing examiners' unpredictable questions, and the inadequacy of practicing alone because nobody in your life can realistically role-play a demanding professor.

This problem is real, it's widespread, and millions of students face it every single year. Now, AI can fill that gap — a simulator that asks tough critical questions just like a real examiner would, complete with feedback on the answers given.

### Finding a language learning partner

Many people want to become fluent in English, Japanese, French, or another language — but run into one consistent obstacle: no reliable conversation partner. Online classes have scheduling constraints. Language exchange apps take time to find the right match, and those matches rarely stick.

This is where AI steps in: a patient, endlessly available conversation partner that never gets tired, never judges, and adjusts its difficulty to match where you are.

> **The common thread in both:** it's not the technology that matters most — it's the clarity of the problem being solved. Both solutions came from the same question: *"What problem do people face every day that still has no satisfying answer?"*

## Why "Small" Problems Are the Most Interesting

There's a long-standing assumption that successful products need to target big, complex problems. Disrupt an industry. Reshape a sector. But experience — and a lot of research — suggests the opposite.

It's the problems that seem trivial, the ones that make people let out a quiet sigh during their daily routine, that are felt most universally and adopted most rapidly when a solution finally appears.

- **Shorter adoption cycles** — people immediately understand the problem without needing a lengthy explanation.
- **Easier to validate** — you can find your first dozen users within days, not months.
- **Stronger word of mouth** — when someone feels the benefit, they instinctively want to tell someone.

Large-scale problems like climate change or healthcare reform matter enormously, but their solution cycles are long. Meanwhile, problems like *"I never know what to say when my bank calls me"* or *"I can't figure out how to professionally apologize to my manager"* — those can be solved next week, and people feel the difference immediately.

## What I'm Looking For Right Now

I'm actively collecting real problems from people with a wide range of backgrounds — not through formal questionnaires with preset choices, but through honest conversations about the things that make life harder than it needs to be.

There are no category restrictions. It could be about work, education, relationships, personal finances, mental health, or things that are even difficult to put into words. The more specific the story, the more useful the contribution.
